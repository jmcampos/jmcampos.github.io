why permaculture?

Permaculture is a system of agricultural and social design principles centered around simulating the patterns and features observed in natural ecosystems. It integrates land, resources, people and the environment through mutually beneficial synergies, and has many branches that include ecological design, ecological engineering, environmental design and construction. Permaculture also includes integrated water resources management that develops sustainable architecture, and regenerative and self-maintained habitat and agricultural systems modelled from natural ecosystems.
One of the founders, David Holmgren, built this system around twelve design principles.

observe and interact

By taking the time to engage with nature we can design solutions that suit our particular situation.

catch and store energy

By developing systems that collect resources when they are abundant, we can use them in times of need.

integrate rather than segregate

By putting the right things in the right place, relationships develop between those things and they work together to support each other.

use small and slow solutions

they are easier to maintain than big ones, making better use of local resources and produce more sustainable outcomes.

use and value renewable resources

Make the best use of nature’s abundance to reduce our consumptive behavior and dependence on non-renewable resources.

use and value diversity

Diversity reduces vulnerability to a variety of threats and takes advantage of the unique nature of the environment in which it resides.

creatively use and respond to change

We can have a positive impact on inevitable change by carefully observing and then intervening at the right time.

produce no waste

By valuing and making use of all the resources that are available to us, nothing goes to waste.

soil matters

Soil needs to be considered as an investment to be protected and cared for. Soil health has a direct impact on human health, and if it is not treated with respect, it will be gone for good. As many have said before, civilization has its roots in the soil, and without soil there will be no future life.

No till farming

Tilling creates soil erosion and releases more carbon into the athmosphere. When tiller tines tear into the soil, they destroy nature’s infrastructure to a healthy soil food web. The undisturbed soil consists of a network of billions of beneficial organisms from bacteria and fungi, nematodes, arthropods and insects, and of course earthworms. Heavy farming equipment compacts soils.

Apply mulch in your soil

Applying organic mulch increases the organic matter in the soil. Microorganisms that are naturally present in the soil degrade the mulch depositing decomposed organic material at the plants’ roots. This organic material provides necessary nutrients and holds moisture in the soil for the plants.

Crop rotation

This is a farming practice which involves growing different types of crops in one location sequentially. This practice reduces soil erosion, increases the soil fertility and subsequently crop yield. Soil degradation leads to erosion and biodiversity loss, and it threatens our food supply.

Plant more trees

Planting trees as a way to combat erosion, to provide protection from wind and rain, dramatically improve stability and stop soil being literally washed away in front of farmers’ eyes. We need to see more trees on our farm landscape and an agricultural regime supporting multi use sustainable agriculture.

Don't use chemicals

Pesticides and chemical fertilizers change the consistency of soil and destroy organisms that aerate the soil. disrupts the natural relationship between plants and microorganisms. Rather than receiving nutrients from the soil and its microbes, plants are force-fed synthetic compounds — often derived from petroleum — that are designed to maximize plant growth and yield. Think of this as similar to feeding a human being a towering pile of pizzas: The plants get more nutrients than they need in a year, and it hardly comes from the most nutritious food.

start composting

Compost is one of nature’s best mulches and soil amendments. Most gardeners know the value of this rich, dark, earthy material in improving the soil and creating a healthful environment for plants. Understanding how to make and use compost is in the public interest, as the problem of waste disposal continues to grow.

Good Examples

One of the main goals of adopting permaculture principles is to empower individuals to be their own producers and move away from being dependent consumers. These techniques are practiced in very different ways globally based on climate and resources particular to the region. While there is variety in the methodology of permaculture practices, the holistic approach remains constant. The easy to remember primary ethics of permaculture include earth care, people care, and resource share.

the case of todmorden

In 2007 a group of individuals, including Pam Warhurst and Mary Clear, created a social movement called Incredible Edible. They wanted a way that everyone could help improve their own community. The answer was food. Everyone understands food.

la Ferme du Bec Hellouin

The Bec Hellouin farm was created by Perrine and Charles Herve-Gruyer in 2006. They took on the principles of permaculture and experimented and developed innovative methods which appear to be particularly productive.

Findhorn Ecovillage

This Ecovillage is a synthesis of some of the very best of current thinking on sustainable human settlements. It is a constantly evolving model used as learning environment by a number of university and school groups as well as by professional organisations and municipalities worldwide.
